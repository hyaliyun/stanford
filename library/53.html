The natural superiority of mistresses and masters and the concomitant inferiority of the men, women and children working for them was incessantly demonstrated in ever new dramatics. State authority made them part and parcel of the colonial construction of law and order, thereby integrating the legal inferiority of natives in the world of modern law, which had been created in Europe and the USA through revolutionary processes. By regulating the status of the native, state power made the violent conquest a permanent condition. And like any administration, the colonial state administration was also designed to function as a mechanism that did not constantly require new decisions to function. It somehow worked independently of the personal characteristics of its personnel. That this objective of bureaucratising rule could not be fully achieved in the colonies need not be explained here. After the formal end of slavery, the colonial state no longer defined locals as things, but in many European colonies the right to exist in their native countries was granted to them only with reservations. In French colonies, this reservation was contained in the collection of laws first published for Algeria (in 1875) and (with minor changes) later for other French colonies. At first it had been entitled ‘Arrêté général sur les infractions de l’indigénat’, while later it was more tersely entitled the ‘Code de l’indigénat’, and very shortly afterwards it came to be contemptuously ‘code matraque’, the law of the truncheon (Le Cour Grandmaison 2010, p. 79). This text had been preceded by a decree issued as early as 1842, which was expressly designed to protect the interests of France and the settlers. In his dissertation submitted in 1905, Jules Vernier de Byans explained with sufficient clarity what this meant tangibly: In order to protect the prestige of the whites, one is often forced to punish an offence committed by a native against a white person more severely than a corresponding offence committed by a white person against a native (de Byans, p. 132f., quoted in Le Cour Grandmaison 1910, p. 72). De facto, from 1842 onwards any conviction for imprisonment for more than six months also implied being transferred to a prison in France. In 1878 and 1879, such deportation was maintained for all sentences exceeding three years. The exceptional character of colonial criminal law was introduced and maintained in many individual provisions, but also in the very organisation of the judicial system itself. Unlike in France, judges could be removed from the colonies (until 1921) if they did not tow the political line. And, of course, nothing of the separation of powers fought for in France was to be found in the colonies. The judiciary acted both as an extended arm of the administration and of the military, especially in cases of collective liability. In concrete terms, criminal law was used to collect taxes by force, to enforce labour obligations, such as the obligation to provide porter services, or to restrict the freedom of movement by criminalising any migration or journey that had not previously been authorised. And as late as the twentieth century, hostages were still taken, and harvests and entire villages destroyed if the taxes demanded had not been delivered on time and in full (Le Cour Grandmaison 2010, p. 84). The administration ordered the large-scale expropriation of land as a punishment for riots - and as a way to gain more land for settlers. The fact that the criminal law enacted for the subjects in the colonies was an exceptional law became particularly clear in the practice of internment. Internment was not imposed by a court, but by an administrative authority, often the governor. Internment was always pronounced for an indefinite period, so that it had a beginning but no known end. And no appeals could be made against such a decision. Initially practised only in Algeria, within a few years the internment measure became an ordinary means of French colonial policy, often used against indigenous populations. In 1904 it was decreed that internment, which always entailed the expropriation of all property, could not be imposed for a period of more than ten years. Gabriel Angoulvant, Governor of Côte d’Ivoire since 1908, stated that only the leaders of revolting natives were interned. In 1911 and 1912, sometimes one, but more often two or more members of a tribe or an administrative unit (cercle) were deported to another French colony. The duration of the internment varied between one and ten years. The deportees were handed out between twenty and fifty francs a month for food, at least officially (Angoulvant 1916, pp. 232-7). Those who returned after the end of their deportation are said to have declared that they ‘wanted to live in good relations with us in the future’ (ibid, p. 238, transl.). When the former Governor of Côte d’Ivoire wrote his report on the methods and results of the ‘pacification’ of this colony, he did not mince his words when assessing official colonial policy. Not only did he criticise the illusion that a country could be colonised peacefully or even through trade, he also considered internment an inappropriate measure. Strictly speaking, the deportations, because they were nothing else, were used to get rid of the leaders or fetish priests who used their influence ‘to keep us from occupying the country’. But even ten years in deportation was in his view inadequate punishment for someone who had rebelled ‘against us’ for the second, third or fourth time. According to him, in point of fact, many people, often an entire tribe, were involved in murders of porters in the service of the colonial power, or suppliers for the colonial army or for Europeans. But according to colonial laws enacted for West Africa, only the leaders could be punished. By comparison, in Indochina, a criminal court had been created for Indochina in 1906, which was able to quickly conduct special court proceedings and to apply the death penalty. He further remarked that although many died during internment in climatically unhealthy areas, he was reluctant to impose such slow deaths. ‘Slow death is not a provision of our laws and should not be accepted; we should have the courage to make the rebels disappear officially and publicly using normal legal means’ (Angoulvant 1916, p. 240, transl.). Contrary to Angoulvant’s assumption, this provision does not seem to have been made use of in Indochina extensively. According to Georges Garros, in the 1920s hundreds were interned every year on an island under terrible conditions (1926, p. 86). On 7 March 1944, indigenous Algerians were granted the same rights and obligations as all other French citizens. This would have made the internment measure illegal had it not been for the fact that, as early as 1939, the possibility of internment of all persons suspected of endangering the defence of the country or public order had been ordered even in France. This law was repealed after the Second World War. But on 26 July 1957, this legal situation was once again brought to an end. After mass demonstrations in Algiers, the possibility of detention in France was introduced, and the respective camps came to be known as ‘centres d’hébergement’ (accommodation centres) (Le Cour Grandmaison 2010, p. 111). Alongside internment, it was above all the collective responsibility for actions committed in opposition to colonial rule that determined the character of the Code de l’Indigénat as a law of exception. For decreeing that it was possible to impose collective punishment, such as on Kabyle tribes in 1871,114 turned the practice of conquest into a permanent condition. The authority to decree criminal provisions for the colonies was vested in the German Emperor, but was transferred by him to the governors and top colonial officials. The colonial administration of justice was only in charge of the white population, while the ‘administration of law for the indigenous’ (Einge-borenenrechtspflege) was the responsibility of the administration. The fact that the governors were empowered to issue them would have stood in the way of unification, if it had been sought. 通过规定土著的地位，国家权力使暴力征服成为一种永久状态。就像任何行政管理一样，殖民地国家的行政管理也旨在作为一个不需要不断决策就能运作的机制。它某种程度上独立于其人员的个人特质而运作。这里无需解释为何这一官僚化统治的目标在殖民地上无法完全实现。 在正式结束奴隶制之后，殖民地国家不再将当地人定义为物品，但在许多欧洲殖民地中，只有带有保留条款，他们才被允许在自己的祖国中存在。 在法国殖民地，这一保留条款包含在首次为阿尔及利亚（1875年）发布的法律汇编中，稍后（经过少量更改）用于其他法国殖民地。起初它被标题为《印第安人违规综合令》，后来被更简洁地称为《印第安人法典》，不久之后就被轻蔑地称为「警棍法典」（Le Cour Grandmaison 2010, 第79页）。这段文本之前的一项法令早在1842年就颁布，明确旨在保护法国和定居者的利益。在1905年提交的博士论文中，朱尔斯·弗尼耶·德·比昂斯以足够的清晰度解释了这意味着什么：为了保护白人的威望，人们往往被迫对土著对白人犯下的罪行处以比白人对土著犯下相应罪行更严重的惩罚（de Byans, 第132f页，引述自Le Cour Grandmaison 1910, 第72页）。事实上，从1842年开始，任何被判处超过六个月监禁的定罪都意味着被转移到法国的监狱。在1878年和1879年，所有超过三年的判决都维持了这种流放。殖民地刑法的特殊性在许多个别条款中被引入并保持，也在司法系统的组织本身中得以保持。与法国不同的是，法官可以（直到1921年）因不遵循政治路线而从殖民地被撤职。当然，在殖民地中找不到在法国争取来的权力分立。司法机构既作为行政机构的延伸，也作为军事机构的延伸，尤其是在集体责任案件中。具体来说，刑法被用来强制征税，执行劳动义务，比如提供搬运服务的义务，或通过将未经事先授权的迁移或旅行定罪来限制自由流动。直到20世纪晚期，如果规定的税款没有按时足额交付，仍会采取劫持人质，破坏收成和整个村庄的措施（Le Cour Grandmaison 2010, 第84页）。为了镇压暴动，行政管理部门命令大规模征用土地作为惩罚——同时也是为定居者获得更多土地的方式。 殖民地为其臣民制定的刑法是一种特例法，在拘留的实践中尤其明显。拘留不是由法院，而是由行政当局，通常是州长决定的。拘留总是无限期地宣布，因此它有开始但没有已知的结束。而且不能对这样的决定提出上诉。最初只在阿尔及利亚实行的拘留措施，在几年内成为法国殖民政策的常规手段，常用于对付土著居民。1904年规定，拘留（总是伴随着所有财产的征用）不能被强加超过十年。加布里埃尔·安古尔万特（Gabriel Angoulvant），自1908年起担任科特迪瓦的州长，表示只有叛乱的土著领袖被拘留。在1911年和1912年，有时一个，但更常见的是一个部落或行政单位（圆）的两个或更多成员被流放到另一个法国殖民地。拘留的时间在一年到十年之间变化。被流放者每月至少官方地获得二十到五十法郎的食物补助（Angoulvant 1916, 第232-7页）。据说那些在流放结束后返回的人宣称他们「希望将来与我们保持良好的关系」（同上，第238页，翻译）。当科特迪瓦的前州长就这个殖民地的“和平化”方法和结果撰写报告时，他在评估官方殖民政策时直言不讳。他不仅批评了可以和平或通过贸易殖民一个国家的幻想，还认为拘留是一种不当的措施。严格说来，流放，因为它们不外乎就是如此，被用来摆脱那些利用其影响力「阻止我们占领该国」的领袖或巫师。但在他看来，对于那些「反对我们」的第二次、第三次或第四次叛乱的人来说，即使是十年的流放也是不够的惩罚。根据他的观点，实际上，很多人，经常是整个部落，参与了对为殖民权力服务的搬运工或为殖民军队或欧洲人供货的谋杀。但根据为西非制定的殖民地法律，只有领袖们可以受到惩罚。相比之下，1906年为印度支那创建了一个刑事法院，能够快速进行特别法庭程序并适用死刑。他进一步指出，尽管许多人在气候不健康的地区的拘留中死亡，他不愿意强加这种缓慢的死亡。「缓慢的死亡不是我们法律的规定，也不应被接受；我们应该有勇气使用正常的法律手段公开正式地使叛乱者消失」（Angoulvant 1916, 第240页，翻译）。与Angoulvant的假设相反，这种措施似乎在印度支那并没有被广泛使用。据乔治·加罗斯（Georges Garros）称，20世纪20年代每年有数百人在恶劣条件下被拘留在一个岛上（1926，第86页）。 1944年3月7日，阿尔及利亚的土著居民被授予与所有其他法国公民相同的权利和义务。如果不是因为早在1939年，即使在法国也已经下令拘留所有被怀疑危及国防或公共秩序的人，那么这本应使拘留措施变得非法。这项法律在第二次世界大战后被废除。但在1957年7月26日，这种法律状况再次结束。在阿尔及尔发生大规模示威后，引入了在法国进行拘留的可能性，相关营地被称为「收容中心」（Le Cour According to Wiener, the historical possibility for constitutional proceedings in favour of non-whites arose after the bloody wars against locals in Queensland had secured white dominance and this was additionally secured by restricting the immigration of Asian workers (200g, p. 70). On the basis of a racial domination set up by means of violence, the rule of law, understood here as a practice of regulated court proceedings, could sometimes also be practised in the border regions of Australia. The situation was different in India. When the East Indian Company began to establish itself in India, it formally ruled on behalf of the Grand Mogul. At the same time, it had been authorised by the English Crown to enact laws and regulations. However, this legislative competence was subject to the obligation of non-repugnance. It was not to be exercised in contradiction with the laws, statutes and customs of the kingdom.123 While this requirement of the Merchants of London, who were listed by name in 1600, was found to be more of a passing remark on trade in East India and the institutional internal structure of the trading company, it nevertheless was already established that all these ‘reasonable laws, constitutions, orders or ordinances’ should also apply to all factors, masters (of ships) and other officers who were in the service of the company. Later, these individuals were grouped together in India under the term officials and thus distinguished from non-officials, i.e. Europeans who were in India as planters, interlopers (smugglers) or in some other private function. The charter renewed by Charles II in 1661 (following the English Revolution) was more precisely formulated. It gave the EIC complete legal authority over all persons living in its territory, regardless of their origin.124 In keeping with the special, inherently contradictory structure that sovereignty in India had taken on during the reign of the East India Company in the course the eighteenth century, not only a patchwork rug of law was created (Hussain 2003, p. 43), but also a patchwork rug of jurisdiction. First there were the royal courts, which were responsible for all inhabitants of Calcutta and for all Europeans in the rural regions (mofussil). In addition, there were the courts of the commercial company for the rural regions, in whose jurisdiction native traditions and regulations of the commercial company entertained a more or less hazardous relationship. With regard to all aspects of family life, i.e. virtually all aspects of material reproduction and domestic dependency and command, local law was meant to be preserved. This included, as mentioned above, slave work in households. Only the burning of widows and the killing of infants would no longer go unpunished, and attacks on travellers and their property were also to be punished more severely in the future. The judges were employees of the East India Company, many of whom had had no legal training. For their rulings, they took into account any principles of common law which they happened to have heard of, the new and often rapidly changing regulations of the trading company and domestic law. In the Company Courts, formalities were largely dispensed with, but British officials repeatedly demanded that Indians appear before them without shoes. Appearing just like a European by wearing shoes was interpreted as a pretence of equality, and consequently as arrogance. Until 1832 Islamic jurists were appointed as jurors in the courts of the trading company. While they were not always able to enforce the fatwa rules, at least they were able to make them heard in proceedings. However, their subordinate role was also made clear by the fact that they were only allowed to sit down during a court hearing if they had been expressly granted permission to do so (Singha 1998/2000, pp. 294F; 291Q. If British officials discriminated against locals in courts, they were expressing the fact that they were representatives of an institution that claimed to rule over locals. But such discrimination differed from the structural legal inequality established in India once the purpose of colonial rule had changed. As long as this purpose had been primarily to secure the appropriation of tributes, attempts by an actual government had remained limited. At the turn of the eighteenth to nineteenth centuries, however, India was increasingly perceived of as a potential market for the supply of raw materials and the sale of British products. In 1813 the trading monopoly of the Eic was abolished and free trade was decided upon for the Indian market. (In 1833 the company was forbidden from engaging in any trade.) Since then the East India Company was keen to govern India. Thus India became an experimental field for ‘civilising’ colonially oppressed natives (Hussain 2003, p. 60). Both within the colonial state and in the mother country, the controversy raised as to whether it was better to establish a strictly authoritarian rule over a population who were accustomed to such domination or to enforce modern government institutions and British concepts of property (Hussain 2003, p. 57) Those who achieved success were those who wanted the export of the rule of law to be understood not only as the most important gift of British colonial power to India and the world, but also as the best instrument for preparing the country for better economic exploitation. Nasser Hussain has well demonstrated that this was not so much a question of introducing certain contents of English law as of establishing legal procedures (2003, p. 65 and passim). It brought about a change from de facto to formally institutionalised legal inequality. The former was mainly due to the fact that the trading company was at best able to control its own employees, but not all other Europeans who were in India. When the London Board of Governors commissioned a report on all Europeans who had been in India between 1766 and 1824 and had been expelled for improper conduct, the report on ‘European Misconduct in India 1766-1824’ contained 25 volumes totalling nearly 20,000 pages (cited in Kol- sky 2010, p. 43). And that was just the report from Bengal. The reports from Madras and Bombay, which were also compiled, were never submitted to the Board of Governors. According to Elizabeth Kolsky, most of those listed in this report as offenders, deported by company at its expense, were vagabonds, robbers, escaped prisoners, deserted soldiers and sailors, planters or fraudsters who were unwilling or incapable of regular work. They posed a threat to Indians, to other Europeans and to the Eic s, the latter mainly because their way of life made it clear that the colonial state was unable to enforce law and order (2010, p. 44). When Warren Hastings, Governor General of India from 1773 to 1785, was questioned on colonial policy by a committee of the House of Lords, he stated that these men had confused their national origin with the right to dominate. Inappropriately, they said ‘We became masters of the country’ (Basu 1925, p. 31; quoted in Kolsky 2010, p. 37). Deportations were not only costly, they were often difficult to manage because the captains of merchant ships refused to take the convicts on board. Consequently, the company had to charter special deportation ships. After some time, however, many of the deportees reappeared in India (Kolsky 2010, p. 47). The virtually impossibility for the trading company to bring non-officials to court was on the one hand an expression of de facto legal inequality, but at the same time also an expression of formal legal inequality institutionalised in various jurisdictions of courts. For the Company Courts in rural areas were only able to proceed against Europeans for unlawful attacks on property or for assault. The royal courts in the administrative centres (presidencies) were exclusively responsible for crimes. This meant, however, that it was hardly possible for natives to file suit against a European. Not only did the defendants have to be taken to distant administrative centres but witnesses also had to be willing and able to stay in court for what could amount to weeks on end. At best, only very wealthy natives ever had the chance to have justice done before such a court (Kolsky 2010, p. 93).”翻译为中文

印度的情况则不同。当东印度公司开始在印度稳固脚跟时，它名义上代表大莫卧儿统治。同时，它被英格兰王冠授权颁布法律和规章。然而，这种立法能力是受到不得违反的义务的限制。它不得与王国的法律、法令和习俗相矛盾。1600年被点名列出的伦敦商人的这一要求，更多地被视为对东印度贸易和贸易公司内部结构的一次权宜之计，但已经明确，所有这些“合理的法律、宪法、命令或条例”也应适用于所有在公司服务的因素、船长（船只）和其他官员。后来，这些人在印度被统称为官员，从而区别于非官方人员，即那些以种植者、私运者（走私者）或其他私人职能身份在印度的欧洲人。查理二世在1661年（继英国革命之后）更新的宪章更为精确地制定。它授予东印度公司对其领地内所有人的完整法律权力，无论他们的出身如何。 在东印度公司统治时期的印度形成的特殊内在矛盾结构下，不仅形成了法律的拼布地毯（Hussain 2003，第43页），还形成了管辖权的拼布地毯。首先是皇家法院，负责加尔各答的所有居民以及农村地区（mofussil）的所有欧洲人。此外，还有商业公司在农村地区的法院，在该法院的管辖范围内，土著传统和商业公司的规定保持着或多或少危险的关系。关于家庭生活的所有方面，即实际上所有物质再生产和家庭依赖与命令的方面，都意味着要保留当地法律。如上所述，这包括家庭中的奴隶工作。只有烧死寡妇和杀害婴儿将不再免受惩罚，对游客及其财产的攻击也将在将来受到更严厉的惩罚。法官是东印度公司的雇员，其中许多人没有接受过法律培训。在作出裁决时，他们会考虑到他们偶然听说过的任何普通法原则、贸易公司不断变化且经常迅速变化的规定和国内法。在公司法院，形式主义在很大程度上被省略，但英国官员反复要求印度人不穿鞋出庭。穿鞋出现得像一个欧洲人被解释为平等的伪装，因此被视为傲慢。直到1832年，伊斯兰教法学者在贸易公司的法院被任命为陪审员。虽然他们并不总是能够执行法塔娃规则，但至少他们能够在程序中使其被听到。然而，他们的从属角色也被明确表达，因为他们只有在被明确允许的情况下才能在法庭听证会期间坐下（Singha 1998/2000，第294F；291Q页）。 如果英国官员在法庭上歧视当地人，他们就是在表达他们是代表声称统治当地人的机构的代表这一事实。但这种歧视不同于一旦殖民统治的目的发生变化后在印度建立的结构性法律不平等。只要其目的主要是确保征税的占有，实际政府的尝试就保持有限。然而，在18世纪末到19世纪初，印度越来越被视为潜在市场，用于供应原料和销售英国产品。1813年，东印度公司的贸易垄断被废除，决定对印度市场自由贸易。（1833年，公司被禁止从事任何贸易。）自那时起，东印度公司渴望治理印度。因此，印度成为了“文明”殖民地被压迫的土著的实验场（Hussain
