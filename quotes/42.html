According to Robbins, transformational leaders not only inspire their followers but also provide moral support and guidance, ensuring the interests of all stakeholders are secured (Zareei, 2014). This underscores the transformative impact of leadership on the organizational environment. Transformational leadership's correlation with follower performance, attitude, and well-being is widely acknowledged. It is recognized as a strategic and effective approach to navigate the unpredictable and dynamic organizational challenges prevalent in today's environment (Nilsen and Cleal, 2011). As organizations grapple with the imperative to adapt to change, transformational leadership emerges as a crucial framework to address the evolving needs of leadership in the contemporary business landscape. Organizational Culture Evolution: A Holistic Perspective In the not-so-distant past, organizational managers concentrated on stewardship and overseeing six pivotal factors influencing productivity: workforce, capital, technology, raw materials, and marketing and management skills. Contemporary management experts assert that organizational culture stands as a paramount factor in shaping and fortifying these elements. Influential figures like Barnard, Selznick, Peters, and Waterman argue that the fundamental task of management is to mold core values and organizational culture, with leadership playing a central role in values management within the organization (Zareei Matin, 1999). Organizational culture, operating beneath the surface, proves crucial in elucidating perplexing issues in the organizational cycle (Suzuki, 2013). The inception of organizational culture traces back to the birth of organizations, with each organization fostering a unique culture shaped by the interaction between founders' principles and organizational members' learning experiences. Edgar Schein, a prominent scholar on organizational culture, defines culture as a set of basic principles and solutions to global problems, evolving over time and transmitted from one generation to the next (Poor Shafeei et al., 2012). Louis, another renowned figure in organizational culture, describes it as a set of perceptions and common understandings guiding organizational actions through language and symbolic tools (Poor Shafeei et al., 2012). Organizational culture, a complex phenomenon, determines the social status within the organization and employees' job positions, presenting challenges in precise definition due to its nuanced nature (Mohelska and Sokolovab, 2015). The Imperative for Organizational Culture Change: Culture, defined as the spirit and social energy of an organization, possesses the potential to propel or impede organizational progress, influencing employee behavior. Any organizational change is intrinsically linked to the existing organizational culture (Soleimani et al., 2012). Widespread changes in organizations, driven by external environmental shifts, necessitate organizational responses to prevent gradual decline. Historical data on large companies underline the urgency for adaptability, with only a fraction surviving over time (Mohelska and Sokolovab, 2015). Over the past two decades, organizations implemented transformative initiatives such as Total Quality Management (TQM), downsizing, and re-engineering. However, these organizations faced defeat, integrity loss, or substantial losses due to the absence of change in organizational culture. The failure to address organizational culture as a fundamental aspect renders tools, techniques, and strategic changes ineffective in improving organizational performance (Giritli et al., 2013). Cultivating cultural change is a challenging, long-term effort, demanding attention to every aspect of the organization to ensure alignment with the desired culture. The 7 S Model, introduced by Vaterman et al. The intricate nature of organizational culture defies a singular theory or framework for identification and study. The Denison Organizational Culture Model (Denison, 2000) emerges as a popular and adaptable model, chosen by many organizations to identify strengths, weaknesses, and developmental aspects of their organizational culture. The model's adaptability to changing circumstances in an organization's external environment, coupled with its depiction of the relationship between environmental requirements, strategic emphasis, and organizational culture, renders it valuable. Denison's model, featuring a two-dimensional matrix representing the degree of change or stability in the job environment and the type of strategic focus (internal or external), includes four dimensions for organizational culture change (Kialashki and colleagues, 2013). Characteristics of Christian Transformational Leadership In delving into the nuanced understanding of Christian Transformational Leadership, it is imperative to go beyond generic definitions and consider the major features consistently found in various texts. This section aims to extract these commonalities from the literature, amalgamating them into a comprehensive definition. 5.6.1. Christian Essence: Undoubtedly, Christian Transformational Leadership is inherently Christian, and a closer examination is needed to elucidate the specific connotations of "Christian" in this context. The initial inquiry revolves around whether Christian Transformational Leadership, being inherently Christian, is centered around Christ. To ascertain this, an exploration of the literature was conducted, focusing on whether Jesus Christ is regarded either as the quintessential model of leadership or as the Object of faith (Stortz, 2008:5). While a considerable body of literature indeed presents Jesus Christ as a model for Christian leadership (Banks and Ledbetter, 2004:111; Barna, 1997:19; Blackaby and Blackaby, 2001; Clinton, 1988:195; Engstrom, 1976:37; Ford, 1993:11; Halcomb, Hamilton, and Malmstadt, 2000:48; Jinkins, 2002:xiii; Wofford, 1999:16) [13], it is crucial to note that this perspective is not universally embraced within the realm of Christian Transformational Leadership. Surprisingly, a substantial portion of the literature does not specifically emphasize Jesus as a model for leadership (Gibbs, 2005; Guder, 1998; Hunter, 2004; Hybels, 2002; Maxwell, 1998; Munroe, 2005; Roxburth and Romanuk, 2006; Sanders, 1994; Stanley, 2003; Stanley and Clinton, 1992; Thomas, 1999; Thrall, McNicol, and McElrath, 1999). This nuanced exploration unveils the complexity within the Christian Transformational Leadership discourse, where the centrality of Jesus Christ as a leadership model is not universally asserted across the literature. Such divergences highlight the need for a more nuanced understanding of the Christian essence within the broader framework of Transformational Leadership. Halcomb, Hamilton, and Malmstadt (2000:19) assert that God Himself serves as the exemplar of Christian leadership. My subsequent inquiry aimed to discern if Jesus Christ could be perceived as the focal point of faith for Christian leaders. However, this exploration yielded limited results. Although faith was occasionally depicted as a favorable aspect of Christian leadership (Clinton 1988:117; Sanders 1994:51) and, in a few instances, considered foundational to Christian leadership (Engstrom 1976:118; Banks and Ledbetter 2004:31; Wofford 1999:16), it was by no means a universally prevalent feature of Christian Transformational Leadership. Despite the variability in the emphasis on faith, all the selected literature unanimously advocated for a Christian approach to leadership (Barna 1997, Cover; Banks and Ledbetter 2004, Cover; Blackaby and Blackaby 2001:xi; Clinton 1988:2; Engstrom 1976:2; Ford 1993, Cover; Gibbs 2005, Cover; Halcomb, Hamilton, and Malmstadt 2000:4; Hunter 2004:Dust Cover; Hybels 2002:11; Sanders 1994, Cover; Wofford 1999, Cover). Moreover, some works were specifically directed towards the Church (Everist and Nessan 2008, Cover; Guder 1998, Cover; Jinkins Scarborough, ‘Christian Transformational Leadership’ 2002, Cover; Roxburgh and Romanuk 2006:Dust Cover; Stanley 2006:ix), while others declared a Christian or Biblical foundation more generally (Thomas 1999:12; Stanley and Clinton 1992:2; Thrall, McNicol, and McElrath 1999:2) or even a Biblical foundation (Maxwell 1998:iii; Wright 2000, Cover). The sole exception to this trend was Munroe (2005), who, while disavowing a specifically Biblical or Christian commitment, included six pages of Scripture references in his book, predominantly derived from the New Testament (Munroe 2005:290). Despite this deviation, he was deemed a Christian author based on this inclusion. Therefore, for the purpose of this context, 'Christian' is defined as those books that either explicitly declare a Christian or Biblical foundation or are expressly directed to the Church. In the realm of Christian Transformational Leadership, the centrality of the concept of influence cannot be overstated. As Maxwell (1998:17) boldly asserts, "Leadership is influence—nothing more, nothing less." Synonyms such as "moving people" (Blackaby and Blackaby 2001:20), "forming people" (Everist and Nessan 2008:1; Guder 1998:183), or having "an effect on outcomes" (Thrall, McNicol, and McElrath 1999:10) are also used in the literature to encapsulate the essence of influence. Numerous Christian Transformational Leadership authors highlighted in this study underscore the pivotal role of influence in this leadership paradigm (Barna 1997:24; Clinton 1988:101; Engstrom 1976:24; Gibbs 2005:22; Hunter 2004:68; Hybels 2002:127; Munroe 2005:52; Sanders 1994:27; Stanley 2006:139; Thomas 1999:31; Wright 2000:31) (Scarborough, 'Christian Transformational Leadership' 73). The majority of these authors emphasize the one-way flow of influence from a leader to followers (Clinton 1988:178; Everist and Nessan 2008:55; Hunter 2004:31; Maxwell 1998:56; Sanders 1994:27; Stanley 2006:139; Thomas 1999:138; Wright 2000:13) (Scarborough, 'Christian Transformational Leadership' 73). However, a few scholars acknowledge the reciprocal nature of influence, recognizing the impact of followers on the leader (Banks and Ledbetter 2004:127; Gibbs 2005:22). In the context of Christian Transformational Leadership, the foundations predominantly align with Burns (1978:20). The crux of this leadership style revolves around the concept of influence, a term integral to the derivation of the label 'transformational.' Influence, in this context, is viewed as a potent force capable of reshaping individuals' motives as they strive toward a shared goal. This stands in sharp contrast to alternative means of goal attainment, such as manipulation (Everist and Nessan 2008:207; Ford 1991:43; Hunter 2004:108, 187; Munroe 2005:43; Thrall, McNicol, and McElrath 1999:21), coercion (Everist and Nessan 2008:207; Hunter 2004:53), command (Halcomb, Hamilton, and Malmstadt 2000:219), or transaction (Banks and Ledbetter 2004:51). The crux of 'influence' in Christian Transformational Leadership lies in its transformative impact on individuals' motivations, prompting them to pursue goals through internal transformation. Christian Transformational leaders, as indicated by Wofford (1999:17), possess the ability to 'change attitudes,' not only influencing the subjects of discourse but also shaping dreams (Ford 1991:15). On occasions, these leaders aspire to go beyond mere attitudinal shifts, seeking to effect comprehensive transformations at both the individual and community levels (Jinkins 2002:xii). Thus, the essence of this leadership style hinges on the transformative power of influence, steering followers toward a higher, purpose-driven collective vision. In the realm of Christian Transformational Leadership, the importance of persuasiveness is consistently underscored as a crucial element for effective influence (Gibbs 2005:21; Munroe 2005:76; Sanders 1994:27). Distinct from mere influence, which focuses on the method of leadership, persuasiveness places emphasis on the leader's capacity to guide and motivate others, steering them toward uncharted territories (Gibbs 2005:21; Thomas 1999:146; Engstrom 1976:64). The four aspects associated with persuasiveness, as noted in secular Transformational Leadership literature, include idealized influence, inspirational motivation, intellectual stimulation, and individualized consideration (Sosik 2006:18; Yukl 1999:2), collectively referred to as 'charisma' (Bass and Riggio 2006:25). While the specifics of these aspects are not the primary focus here, it is essential to recognize that persuasiveness is a recurring theme in Christian Transformational Leadership literature. Various authors in this study consistently advocate for persuasiveness as a vital trait of the Christian Transformational leader (Barna 1997:23; Banks and Ledbetter 2004:40; Blackaby and Blackaby 2001:17; Clinton 1988:14; Engstrom 1976:64; Ford 1993:25; Halcomb, Hamilton, and Malmstadt 2000:51; Hunter 2004:185; Maxwell 1998:162; Sanders 1994:73; Stanley 2006:118; Stanley and Clinton 1992:145; Wright 2000:18). The capacity to persuade, motivate, and guide others emerges as a consistent and fundamental theme in the discourse on Christian Transformational Leadership. As noted by Maxwell (1998:203), a leader must employ the right actions at the right time to ensure success. Sanders (1994:113) emphasizes the necessity for leaders to utilize tactics that lead to success. Thomas (1999:31) underscores the importance of crucial skills such as timing, creativity, and discipline. Stanley (2003:79) contends that a sound strategy is fundamental for every effective leader, equating it to the approach taken by a skilled coach in a game. Various authors on Christian Transformational Leadership consistently stress the significance of strategy (Barna 1997:25; Banks Ledbetter 2004:133; Blackaby and Blackaby 2001:70; Clinton 1988:88; Everist and Nessan 2008:101; Ford 1993, Cover; Gibbs 2005:99; Guder 1998:201; Halcomb, Hamilton, and Malmstadt 2000:130; Hybels 2002:55; Munroe 2005:243; Thrall, McNicol, and McElrath 1999:181; Wofford 1999:89; Wright 2000:71). Finally, the lack of control data, prevalent in both secular and Christian Transformational Leadership literature, can be addressed. Summary Defining theories of Christian leadership holds paramount importance as it facilitates the distinction between various theories, identifies practitioners, and enables research into their effectiveness. This article aimed to define a specific Christian leadership theory termed Christian Transformational Leadership. The chosen methodology involved commencing with a definition of secular Transformational Leadership, followed by the identification of major characteristics within Christian Transformational Leadership literature, ultimately leading to the extraction of a comprehensive definition for Christian Transformational Leadership. The aspiration is that by providing a more rigorous definition than currently available, this research will contribute to the advancement of the field, particularly in evaluating the efficacy of Christian Transformational Leadership theory. Through a well-defined framework, scholars and practitioners alike can engage in more targeted and insightful exploration of Christian leadership within a transformational context. Transformational leadership has garnered significant academic attention over the past two decades, emerging as a new paradigm for comprehending leadership dynamics. This concept was developed under the guidance of Bernard Bass (1997), defining leadership that emphasizes the imperative for change, the creation of a future vision, and the mobilization of follower commitment to achieve results beyond conventional expectations. The empirical evidence supporting transformational leadership's effectiveness is substantial, with over 100 studies consistently establishing its positive correlation with organizational and leadership effectiveness (Bryman 1992; Lowe, Kroeck, & Sivasubramaniam 1996). These findings hold true across diverse samples and contexts, ranging from Fortune 100 businesses and organizations to military units and presidential administrations. Recent studies have extended the examination of transformational leadership beyond a North American context. For instance, Dorfman and Howell (1996) explored the manifestation of transformational and transactional leadership behaviors in Mexico, as did Yakochi (1989) in Japan, Kuchinke (1999) in Germany, and Ardichvili and Gasparishvili (2001) in Eastern Europe. These studies revealed compelling evidence for the existence of transformational leadership behaviors in each cultural context. Yet, a more in-depth examination of the literature reveals a paucity of research on the effectiveness of transformational leadership in an international context. Given that the genesis of transformational leadership originated in Western culture, it is imperative to discern the extent to which its effectiveness varies depending on individual cultural values. For instance, if a high-potential Taiwanese manager, educated in the West about the advantages of a transformational leadership approach, adopts such behaviors, the question arises: will their boss appreciate these transformational behaviors, or will they perceive them as a threat? In certain non-Western cultures, the change-oriented nature of transformational leaders might be interpreted as a lack of respect for tradition. Anticipated cross-cultural discrepancies are likely to escalate in today's fiercely competitive global business environment. Management vs. Leadership: Appreciating Both What is Management? Management, in its essence, refers to the systematic coordination of resources and tasks to achieve specific organizational goals and objectives. It involves planning, organizing, directing, and controlling resources, including human capital, to ensure efficiency and effectiveness in achieving predetermined outcomes. Managers, often operating within established structures and hierarchies, focus on optimizing processes, maintaining stability, and ensuring that tasks are executed in accordance with set plans and guidelines. What is Leadership? In contrast, leadership encompasses a broader and more dynamic spectrum of influence and inspiration. Leadership involves the ability to motivate, guide, and inspire individuals or groups toward a shared vision or goal. Unlike management, leadership transcends formal positions and is not confined to prescribed roles within an organizational structure. Leaders propel change, cultivate innovation, and navigate uncertainties, drawing on personal qualities, vision, and the capacity to influence others. Defining Leadership: The term "leadership" eludes a precise definition, as its meaning varies across contexts. It can encompass the traits exhibited by those in leadership roles, the functional tasks they perform, or the influence they exert on others. Despite its elusive nature, leadership is a widely observed yet often misunderstood phenomenon. As Rex Jones succinctly put it, "Leadership is what leaders do," emphasizing that it is an action, not merely a position. Leadership involves influencing and shaping the behavior of individuals or groups, as articulated by Norman Shawchuck. Peter Drucker further emphasizes that leadership is not solely about charismatic qualities but is a means to accomplish goals, with its essence deeply rooted in performance. Leadership, according to Drucker, manifests itself in actions that are motivated by a desire to bring about change and achieve shared objectives. James Burns contributes a nuanced perspective, stating that leadership involves mobilizing resources, both institutional and psychological, to satisfy the motives of followers and realize mutually held goals. Burns underscores the relational aspect, highlighting that leadership is a process in which leaders influence followers to act in alignment with shared values, visions, or motivations. Articulating the Distinction: The distinction between leadership and management lies in their primary focuses and approaches. Management revolves around the efficient coordination of resources and tasks within established frameworks, ensuring the accomplishment of predetermined objectives. It is a structured and procedural aspect of organizational functioning, vital for maintaining stability and operational effectiveness. Leadership, on the other hand, is more fluid and visionary. It is about inspiring and influencing others to embrace change, navigate challenges, and work collaboratively toward a common purpose. Leadership often challenges the status quo, fostering innovation and adaptation in response to evolving circumstances. Valuing Both: While leadership and management are distinct, they are not mutually exclusive. In fact, effective organizational governance often requires a harmonious integration of both leadership and management principles. Managers bring stability and order, ensuring that established processes are adhered to, while leaders provide direction, inspire creativity, and propel the organization toward future success. In essence, valuing both leadership and management acknowledges the importance of maintaining organizational stability while fostering innovation and growth. A balanced and synergistic approach, recognizing the unique contributions of each, ensures a holistic and adaptive organizational culture poised for sustained success in a dynamic environment.
